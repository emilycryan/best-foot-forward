Pain management is the first protocol that actually matters. This page tracks what I take, in what order, and how I step down off it.

## The hardest rule first: no NSAIDs

All NSAIDs (ibuprofen, Advil) stop seven days before surgery and stay out through the first eight weeks: bleeding risk and slower bone healing. Last time I caved for one emergency week; this time I'm toughing it out. [The full eulogy's in the journal](/entries/2026-06-18-my-precious).

## OxyContin first, then oxycodone

The opioid plan runs in two stages, and they're the same drug wearing different clothes. **OxyContin** is the extended-release formulation: one dose holds for about 12 hours, so it lays down a steady baseline through the worst of the 96-hour rebound window. **Oxycodone** is the immediate-release version of that exact same molecule: it hits faster but only lasts about 4 hours, making it the as-needed tool for pain spikes once the round-the-clock need eases. Start on the -contin, step down to the -codone. [The why is in the journal](/entries/2026-06-25-comfortably-numb).

## Hydroxyzine: the Doublemint gum drug

<img class="float-right xs" src="/entries/hydroxy.jpeg" alt="My hand holding the prescription bottle: hydroxyzine HCl 25 mg tablets, one by mouth every six hours as needed." />

Double your pleasure, double your fun: [hydroxyzine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682866.html) is one drug with two jobs. Taken with the opioids, it "gives it a boost" (my doctor's words), tuning the pain relief to 11 without adding more opioid. Taken alone, it works like prescription-strength Benadryl, which matters when the post-surgical itchfest kicks in a week or so after surgery and the incisions, the wraps, and the leg itself all start screaming at once. If your surgeon offers it, take the script. [The itchfest is in the journal](/entries/2026-07-16-itchycoo-park).
